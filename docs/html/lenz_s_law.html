Lenz's law

Lenz's law is a scientific law under electromagnetism that was formulated in 1834 by the physicist Emil Lenz. Lenz's law goes:The current induced in a circuit due to a change in a magnetic field is directed to oppose the change in flux and to exert a mechanical force which opposes the motion. The equation for Lenz's law is comprised of the following terms:
&eps - The electromagnetic force
N - The number of turns in the coil
&der - The partial derivative
&mphis-bB - The magnetic flux
t - Time
The equation reads:The electromagnetic force is equal to the negative of the product between the number of turns in the coil and the change of the magnetic flux divided by the change over time. Lenz's law explains how when a magnetic field is changed near a conductor, a current is induced in the conductor. This induced current generates a magnetic field that is opposite to the motion of the original magnetic field. The field produced is dependent on the turns in the coil and the rate at which the original field is produced. A stronger magnetic flux over a shorter period of time will induce a stronger opposing field.
